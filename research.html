Galaxy evolution through cosmic time can only be understood by studying the properties of galaxies with multi-wavelength extragalactic survey. In particular, it is known that galaxy evolution is closely related to the large scale environment (the dark matter halos) they reside in, and the evolution of galaxies and the supermassive black holes (SMBHs) in their centers are also closely connected.

To uncover the mechanisms that scale the systems from a few AU to mega-parsec scale, I'm currently working on research projects about the correlation between the supermassive black holes and their host galaxies. My work involves analyzing multi-wavelength observations in the Boötes field, including data from Chandra, Spitzer, Herschel, the AGN and Galaxy Evolution Survey (AGES) catalog and NOAO Deep Wide Field Survey (NDWFS). The goal of my research is to put together a more complete picture on the evolution of galaxies, AGNs and the dark matter halos.

The clues of SMBH-galaxy co-evolution may lie in star-forming galaxies instead AGN-hosting galaxies

Ever since the discovery of the tight correlation between the galaxy bulge mass and the SMBH mass for nearly every massive galaxies, a large number of studies have been trying to look for the underlying physical mechanisms that drive this correlation. The searches so far focused mostly on AGN and AGN host galaxies, where the SMBHs are most actively growing. However, recent simulations (and some observational evidence) have shown that the time scale of the variability of AGN accretion rate is much shorter than the time scale of star-formation.

Therefore, we propose that the we should focus on studying the average SMBH accretion in star-forming galaxies to truly uncover the link between the growth of SMBHs and their host galaxies. Please see my published article for details about this study, or you can find a short summary of this paper at the ``weekly science update'' from Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics

AGN Obscuration and host galaxy star formation

A simple explanation of the close correlation between black hole accretion and star formation is that they are both fueled by the same gas reservoir. This is consistent with a well-established galaxy evolution scenario in which rapid star formation and active SMBH accretion are fueled by events of gas-rich major mergers. In this scenario, galaxies first go through a dust-enshrouded starburst phase with a heavily obscured central AGN. Later on, the powerful radiation from the central AGNs drive the gas and dust away from the galaxies and shut down the star formation.

By studying mid-IR selected, luminous quasars in the Bootes survey region, we find that the far-IR emission in quasar host galaxies is directly connected to the quasar obscuration. With careful SED fitting , X-ray and far-IR stacking analysis, we confirmed that the obscured quasars are hosted by galaxies with at least two times higher star formation rate than the unobscured quasars. This suggests that in addition to the typical ``torus'' AGN unification model, the dust star forming AGN hosts is also a very important source of obscuration. Some of our mid-IR quasars show signs of extrem obscurations in the X-ray and the optical. Since the most rapid growth of galaxy and SMBH happens at this dust-enshrouded phase, it is important to include these heavily obscured AGN population when search for evidence of the AGN-galaxy coevolution. This work is accepted for publication in ApJ and is also available at arXiv.

Are quasars X-ray weaker than we thought?

The growth rate of an SMBH is probably the most important physical parameter in its evolution. Observationally, the SMBHs are believed to grow through the rapid accretion of gas in AGNs. Typically, a radiatively efficient accretion process can efficiently convert 10% of the accreted rest mass into radiation (bolometric). While it is not realistic to expect that we can measure the SMBH accretion rates with a contemporary, panchromatic observation of AGNs, there are many effective AGN accretion rate estimators that are commonly used to convert observables to AGN bolometric luminosity (hence the SMBH accretion rate). In particular, I am investigating two AGN accretion rate indicators: the AGN mid-IR luminosity and the AGN X-ray luminosity. This is crucial because astronomers are just begining to truly understand the ``dust-obscured'' AGNs in the last decade. Since mid-IR might sometimes be the only AGN accretion rate indicator that's available for heavily obscured sources, it is important to understand how well the AGN mid-IR luminosity traces the intrinsic SMBH accretion rate. In my recent study, I choose to study the correlation between AGN mid-IR luminosity and X-ray luminosity, since X-ray is often considered as an excellent SMBH accretion rate indicator.

In local AGNs, (the low to moderate luminosity Seyfert galaxies), this two monochromatic luminosities are almost linearly correlated (Lutz+ 2004, Miaolino+ 2007 and Gandhi+ 2009). However, for the most luminous quasars, different correlations were observed (Fiore+ 2009), which suggests that quasars might have relatively weaker X-ray luminosity when compared to the local X-ray to mid-IR luminosity ratio. Recent studies with NuSTAR have also revealed some quasars to have intrinsically weak X-ray emissions (e.g. Stern+ 2014, Lansbury 2014). (NuSTAR can directly probe the ultra-hard X-ray photons, which are much less susceptible to the neutral gas absorption than the lower energy (<10 keV) X-ray photons)

To explore the nature of AGN accretion rate indicators in quasars, I have compiled several quasar catalogs and confirmed that on average, the AGN X-ray to mid-IR luminosity ratio is indeed lower than the local relation. This is essential in the studies of AGN and galaxy coevolution since the choice of SMBH accretion rate indicators can actually alter the interpretation of the results. (Chen et al. 2015b, to be submitted)
